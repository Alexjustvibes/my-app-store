## Hook (the counterintuitive claim, in one line)
The most useful creator advice online is empirically true and "scientifically" false at the same time — the practice was discovered by doing, then dressed in borrowed neuroscience that nobody checked, including the person selling it.

## Who this is for (the specific, often-overlooked person)
Two overlooked people, pick one:
1. The skeptic who *dismisses* a genuinely good practice because the explanation sounds like woo ("Default Mode Network," "11 million bits per second") — throwing out a working tool because its instruction manual is fiction.
2. The creator who feels they need to wrap their hard-won, lived practice in a study or a brain diagram to be allowed to say it — and weakens it by doing so.

## The argument
- Exhibit A: [[thinkers/dan-koe]] gives a genuinely good writing process, then says, out loud, *"I am not big into psychology. I haven't researched this much, but I've experienced this phenomenon."* See [[sources/dan-koe-creativity-vs-productivity]]. He's telling you the map is borrowed and the territory is real.
- This is the normal structure of practical knowledge, not a scandal: the practice is **derived from experience**; the mechanism is a **post-hoc story** attached for transmission. The story can be wrong while the practice stays true.
- Connects to fallibilism / Deutsch: a good *explanation* and a working *practice* are different objects. We constantly run on practices whose explanations we'd flunk a test on. Demanding the explanation be correct before you'll use the practice is a category error.
- The turn: the honest move isn't to fake better science — it's to *say it's experiential* (Koe does, and it makes him more credible, not less). The dishonest move, which fills the timeline, is borrowing scientific authority you haven't earned to launder a vibe.

Need 2-3 more examples of the same structure (good practice + borrowed/wrong mechanism) so it reads as a pattern, not a one-off dunk on Koe — and the framing must be *generous*, not a gotcha. The strongest version probably ends on a rule: how to tell load-bearing science from decorative science in the advice you consume. That rule is the piece.
