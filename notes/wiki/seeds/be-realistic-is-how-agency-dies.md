# Seed: "Be realistic" is the most agency-destroying sentence ever said to a child

Stage: raw            # raw → validating → newsletter → video
Serves Current Bet: yes

## Hook (the counterintuitive claim, in one line)
The hopelessness you feel about your adult life was installed at around age 8, in a single sentence — when you said what you wanted to do and someone who loved you said "that's not realistic."

## Who this is for (the specific, often-overlooked person)
Two readings (pick one):
- The adult who can't figure out why their stable, "successful" life feels hollow — and has never traced it back to the moment they stopped believing wanting things was allowed.
- The well-meaning parent/mentor who thinks "being realistic" is protection, and is actually the first villain in their kid's story.

## The argument
- The article opens here: at 8 you mention the hobby you'd do for a living, the family says it isn't realistic, you give up — "congratulations, you were born into a low-agency life." From [[concepts/agency]] / [[sources/modern-day-hero-alex-2026-05-24]].
- "Be realistic" feels like care; it functions as the *installation* of low agency. The degree→job path then maintains what that sentence started.
- The cruelty is that it's delivered by people who love you — which is exactly why it lands so deep and goes unexamined for decades. You can't resent it, so you absorb it.
- The turn / what most people get wrong: they think their lack of drive is a personality trait ("I'm just not ambitious"). It's more often a *learned* belief with a datable origin — which means it can be un-learned. Naming the moment is the first move toward agency.

## Open question / what would make this undeniable
This is the most emotionally loaded seed of the batch — strongest as a *story*, weakest as a lecture. Needs the human's own age-8 sentence (or a vivid, specific one), told concretely. The honest counter to pre-empt: sometimes "be realistic" *is* correct — the piece is stronger if it distinguishes killing a dream from pressure-testing one. Avoid blaming parents cheaply; the depth is in how love and harm coexist in the same sentence.
