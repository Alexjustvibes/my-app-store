## Hook (the counterintuitive claim, in one line)
Trading your time for a paycheck isn't the worst theft happening to you — it's the smaller one. The job takes your hours; the phone takes the hours the job left you, so you never even notice you're free at 6pm.

## Who this is for (the specific, often-overlooked person)
The employed person who tells themselves "I'll work on my own thing after work" — and then doesn't, and assumes it's a discipline failure. It isn't only discipline. They're caught in *both* traps at once and only see one.

## The argument
- Two captures, not one ([[concepts/agency]], from [[frameworks/the-jester-hero]] / [[sources/modern-day-hero-alex-2026-05-24]]):
  - **Tax slave** — bartered most of your *time* to someone else's goals.
  - **Mind slave** — your *attention* belongs to the next dopamine hit (social feed).
- The double bind is the whole point: most people are *both*, so even reclaimed time isn't usable. You clock out with hours technically free and a mind too fried to direct them. The "I have no time" story is half-true; the truer story is "I have no *attention*."
- This reframes the failure: you don't need more hours or more willpower first — you need to win back *attention* before time is even worth reclaiming. (Connects to [[concepts/flow]]: no attention, no flow.)
- The turn / what most people get wrong: they fight the time war (quit the job, side hustle at night) while losing the attention war (still doomscrolling), and wonder why freedom doesn't arrive.

## Open question / what would make this undeniable
"Mind slave" is a sticky coinage — lean on it. Needs a concrete attention-audit moment (the bar scene from the article: Henry on his phone the whole time) made vivid, ideally with a real number (hours/day on feeds vs. hours/day on your own goals). Risk: anti-phone content is saturated; the *double-theft* framing (job + phone as a combined system) is what makes this not just another "delete the app" take.
