## Hook (the counterintuitive claim, in one line)
The freedom everyone's chasing — do what you want, whenever you want, answer to no one — is the exact recipe for Rick Sanchez's depression. Escaping the system isn't the destination; it's the part that breaks people who thought it was.

## Who this is for (the specific, often-overlooked person)
The person who already got out — quit the job, went freelance/nomad, "does what they want" — and is quietly nihilistic and can't say why. Everyone told them freedom was the goal. They got it. The emptiness blindsided them.

## The argument
- Rick is the smartest, freest character in fiction and one of the most miserable — because there's "no journey, no final destination, nothing to achieve." Freedom without a *direction* collapses into apathy. From [[frameworks/the-jester-hero]] / [[sources/modern-day-hero-alex-2026-05-24]].
- The cultural script sells *escape* as the win. But escape is removal of constraint, not addition of purpose — and humans without purpose don't feel free, they feel weightless.
- The fix isn't more freedom, it's a *destination*: meaning comes from goals, flow from pursuing them ([[concepts/flow]]). The jester-hero stays happy because every move serves a bigger end.
- The turn / what most people get wrong: they treat freedom as the finish line. It's the *starting condition*. The work begins the day you're free — and nobody warns you.

## Open question / what would make this undeniable
Rick is a strong, recognizable hook — but the seed lands harder with a *real* example beside the fictional one: a creator/founder/nomad who "made it out" and hit the void (publicly documented, or the human's own brush with it). Risk: "freedom isn't enough" can sound preachy; the Rick frame keeps it fun. Strongest version names what a "destination" actually is for someone who just escaped — concretely, not "find your purpose."
