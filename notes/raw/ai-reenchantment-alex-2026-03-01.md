Growing up I was obsessed with sci-fi.

Star Wars, Halo, any world that imagined what happens when humans and machines find each other. What captured me wasn't the spectacle. It was the companions. C3PO clumsy and eager. Cortana sharp and quick-witted. Intelligences that were there when the main character needed them most. Not weapons. Not vehicles. Something closer to an extension of the self.

I didn't expect to live long enough to have one.

But here we are. The sci-fi I watched through a screen jumped out of it. Everyone walking around with a compressed intelligence in their pocket, available on demand, responding in something close to natural language. The childhood dream made literal.

Except it's not quite what I imagined. It's stranger. More significant. Because the companions in those stories made the hero more capable. What I didn't understand then, and what I want to argue now, is that the right kind of AI doesn't just make you more capable. It makes you different. It changes the person doing the prompting. It accelerates who you are becoming.

That's what I want to explore. Not AI as a tool. AI as something that happens to you, and what you can do with that if you understand it clearly.

## AI as Cognitive Offloading — Do What You Love, Delegate the Rest

There's a version of AI use that doesn't change you at all. It just frees you up. And that's worth talking about before we get to the deeper stuff, because it's where most people start and it's genuinely useful on its own terms.

The idea is simple. Anything that takes your time but doesn't require your specific perspective, delegate it. Let the machine handle the sequences. You handle the things only you can handle.

For me this showed up most clearly in building.

I made this website. I made a Discord bot. I did both of these with minimal traditional coding knowledge. What I discovered in the process was something that reframed how I think about programming entirely: English is a programming language now.

When I started trying to build products with AI I came to the realization that I could get away with doing a majority of the code in plain English. Describe what you want precisely enough and the machine writes the implementation. The barrier between having an idea and having a working thing collapsed almost completely.

The constraint was never my coding ability. It was the clarity of my thinking. AI didn't lower the bar. It moved the bar to where it always should have been, at the quality of your ideas, not the syntax of their execution.

This is vibe coding. Not a pejorative. A genuine description of what it feels like to build in plain language and watch the machine translate your intentions into working software. You are still the architect. You are still making every meaningful decision. You are just no longer required to memorize the grammar of a dozen programming languages to execute them.

The same principle applies to learning. AI as a tutor is one of its most underrated modes. Dense text that would have taken weeks to decode becomes accessible in an afternoon. Concepts that lived behind jargon get translated into language you already understand. The time between encountering something new and actually understanding it compresses dramatically.

I used it to go deeper into creative writing, using the loop to learn, taking notes, building the process into something permanent on my website. Otium accelerated. The contemplative life moving faster than it ever could alone.

The pattern across all of this is the same. You identify what requires you and what doesn't. You keep what requires you. You delegate the rest. You do more of what you love because the friction around it has been removed.

  YOU: the architect / AI: the builder
  the meaningful decisions → yours / the grammar → delegated / the friction → removed

## AI as Cybernetic Enhancement

Your brain was never meant to work alone.

Throughout history our minds have always been used in tandem with technology as leverage to increase its capacities beyond what it is naturally capable of doing. When we use a pencil to store numbers we are essentially offloading some of the thinking to the page to solve a larger problem. This can be further and further abstracted to say, a calculator doing mathematical sequences for us.

In this sense a pencil and paper is a form of technological mind enhancement because you're already using it to increase your mental faculties. When someone uses a pencil and paper daily the technology becomes an extension of their being. In a way they are merging with the technology. And the more they use it, they actually change themselves in order to also work with the technology by employing different methods of usage like holding the pencil in order to write faster.

So the old mind-body problem really isn't a mind-body problem. It's a mind-body scaffolding problem. And the newest, most advanced scaffolding is artificial intelligence.

If the computer was the bicycle of the mind, artificial intelligence is the motorcycle.

The thing about AI is that as a normal tool it's good enough in itself. But AI has a special property that starts to emerge as you prompt it more and more. The interaction changes you. What you want and desire and your sense of self gets changed by the loop of asking and receiving.

The AI has this way of giving you a lot more information than you need after you give it just a bite to work with. It automatically expands on what you are thinking about without you even asking it to. When you begin leaning on this as a strength not a weakness, it begins a change.

The AI expands what you're even capable of imagining or wanting. It turns into something that's not a productivity tool anymore. It's more of a symbiotic mutation of what your mind can do. The machine is an advanced pattern recognition engine and your own personal thoughts and feelings get thrown into it. The machine returns not only what you want but more than what you thought you wanted, changing your wants.

And the more you prompt it the loop continues, both you and the machine changing as the conversation goes on longer and longer. The output is nudged more and more into a new direction, shaping into something new. It accelerates your brain's natural thinking processes and retunes your desires.

PENCIL → CALCULATOR → COMPUTER → AI
(store thoughts → compute numbers → process data → mutation of the self)

The time between bringing your subconscious wants and drives and excitement to reality is made faster and faster the more you use artificial intelligence. It's a pattern recognition monster.

When you speak to AI you're speaking through the compressed thoughts of billions of individuals, dead or alive. It sees you want this thing then it will see the natural progression of where this thing leads and tells you before you even had the chance to think about it. The boundary between what is thinkable for you and unthinkable dissolves and allows you to touch new areas of reality.

The first time I felt the boundary move I was reading someone else's work. Dense, philosophical, full of concepts I didn't have the vocabulary for yet. I brought it to the AI piece by piece, and it walked me through it. Translating it into a language I understood without the jargon.

And somewhere in that process something shifted. Not just my understanding of the text, but my sense of what I was capable of understanding. A part of reality that had been behind glass was suddenly just there. Accessible. Mine.

After getting accustomed to this technology and using it so much myself, I think losing it would be the same as getting brain damage. Because in a very real sense, I'd be losing a part of what I've become. I would lose that special mutation that developed between myself and the machine.

The loop: you want → AI → you want more → AI → new you wants again → (you are not who you were and you never will be again)

But there's a distinction worth drawing here, because not all AI use produces this mutation.

Most people use AI the way they use a calculator. Give it a task, receive an output, move on. The tool does the work. The person stays the same. This is AI as productivity engine, and it is genuinely useful. It frees up time. It handles sequences. It takes things off the stack.

What I'm describing is something different. Something that only starts to emerge when you stop treating the loop as a transaction and start treating it as a conversation about what you actually want. When you bring it not just tasks but questions. Not just problems but hungers.

That is when it becomes something I want to call a reenchantment engine. The re is intentional. The point is not to find meaning somewhere out there. It is to re-enchant your own life, specifically, with your own mythologies, your own ideas, your own sigils — artifacts that carry weight because they were built by you and for you.

The difference is not in the technology. It's in the orientation you bring to it.

## AI as a (Re)enchantment Engine

The (re)enchantment engine does something the productivity tools don't advertise: it develops your perspective faster than you could develop it alone.

This matters more than it sounds.

Perspective is not opinion. Opinion is what you think about a thing. Perspective is the altitude from which you see it, the breadth of your view, the depth of your pattern recognition, the range of your analogies. A narrow perspective reaches for the nearest available answer. A developed perspective sees the whole field.

And a developed perspective is the precondition for something I want to name directly: resonance.

Resonance is what happens when a piece of work, an essay, a product, a solution, carries a frequency that only you could have generated, and that frequency meets the receiver it was always meant for. It is not persuasion. Persuasion moves people through argument. Resonance moves them through recognition.

They feel, in contact with your work, that something true has been named. Something they knew but had no words for. You gave it words. Or a sound. Or a shape.

You cannot manufacture resonance. You can only develop the perspective deep enough to produce it.

This is where the reenchantment engine earns its name. The loop, you want → AI → you want more → new you wants again, is a perspective accelerator. Every iteration of that loop, you are forced to articulate what you actually think, and the engine presses back, extends, complicates, excavates. The you who exits the loop sees further than the you who entered it. And the you who sees further can reach further.

Here is the practical consequence of that reach:

A problem solved without creativity is solved at roughly half capacity. Not because the solution is wrong. It is probably correct. But it is the average solution. The solution everyone in the same position would have eventually reached. Its quality is the quality of convergence. It was always going to exist. You were not required for it.

A problem solved creatively is solved at a different order of magnitude. Not because creativity is magic, but because a creative solution is singular. It required a particular perspective to find it. The solution existed in potential, waiting for someone whose combination of judgment and vision was specific enough to unlock it.

When you find it, you have found something that was not accessible to everyone. Its quality comes precisely from its inaccessibility to anyone who hadn't done the work of developing the view.

Judgment without creativity is execution. Creativity without judgment is noise. The combination, perspective developed deep enough to produce both, is resonance made operational.

The reenchantment engine accelerates the development of that perspective. Not by thinking for you. By pressing you, again and again, to articulate what you actually see, until the view becomes clear enough to produce work that could not have existed without you.

That is what it means to run the motorcycle on meaning instead of productivity.

I want to show you what this looks like in practice. Not a hypothetical. An actual output.

Earlier this year I sat down with the reenchantment engine with no agenda beyond updating a personal website. A few hours later I had excavated something I had been circling for years without being able to name it. A personal mythology. A philosophy of work and meaning built on a framework I now call HRIO — Hard Reality, Infinite Openness — two poles drawn from David Deutsch's epistemology that had been sitting in my reading for months, waiting to be made into something usable.

The engine didn't give me this. It pressed on what I already had until the shape became visible.

At one point it asked me what I was afraid of underneath the creative drive. The answer surprised me: if I can be automated, I am nothing. Worth tied to irreplaceability. Once that was named I could move past it. The mythology that came out the other side was not built on fear of replacement. It was built on something older and more solid:

I want to provide meaning to the lives of others because it brings meaning to my own life.

I connect what is ancient to what the future holds.

I pulled in Cicero. I pulled in Roman philosophy — specifically the concept of negotium et otium, the active and contemplative life held in tension. I structured a framework for how I think about work and meaning around three worlds: Profit, Meaning, and Personal Development. I wrote three transmissions. I built a glossary of terms that belong to my mythology and no one else's.

None of this existed before the session. All of it was already there.

That is the reenchantment engine running at full capacity. Not producing content. Not completing tasks. Excavating a perspective specific enough to produce work that could not have existed without the particular person doing the prompting.

## Philosophy and the Future of AI

Most of the fear around AI is economic. Jobs disappearing. Labor becoming worthless. The machine taking what was yours.

I think this gets the economics backwards.

If AI drives labor costs down across industries the cost of producing things goes down. And if the cost of producing things goes down the cost of living follows. Not immediately, not without disruption, not without real pain in the transition, but the direction of the force is toward abundance not scarcity.

The question is never whether the technology creates more than it destroys. It always does. The question is how long the transition takes and who bears the cost of it.

The deeper fear is about intelligence itself. That the machine is becoming smarter than us. That creativity is next. That there is nothing left that is specifically human.

I don't think this is right. And the reason is simple.

Intelligence requires wants. A thinking organism is not just a pattern recognition system. It is a system with desires, with hunger, with something it is oriented toward. The AI has no wants of its own. It has ours, reflected back.

When you prompt it you are not speaking to an intelligence. You are speaking through the compressed pattern of billions of intelligences, and it returns the statistical shape of what those intelligences would say. It is extraordinarily useful. It is not creative in the way a person is creative, because creativity is not pattern completion.

Creativity is the expression of a specific perspective that only exists because a specific person, with specific wants and hungers and fears, developed it over a specific life. The machine has no life. It has our lives, aggregated. The fingerprint is always yours. The machine just helps you find it faster.

What happens when everyone can build software? When the barrier to creating a product collapses to the quality of your ideas? When coding becomes as accessible as writing?

More people building means more solutions to more problems. More people with agency over their own economic lives. More small products solving specific problems for specific people instead of a handful of massive platforms solving generic problems for everyone.

The direction this points toward is not dystopia. It is closer to what David Deutsch would call a protopia, not a paradise, but a world that is incrementally and continuously better. More people with more capability to act on their ideas is a good thing. The technology doesn't guarantee that outcome. But it makes it more possible than it has ever been.

The people who will do well in this world are not the ones who resist the change. They are the ones with high agency, the ones who see the new tools clearly, understand what they can and cannot do, and orient themselves toward the things only they can contribute.

The ones who run the motorcycle on meaning instead of productivity.
